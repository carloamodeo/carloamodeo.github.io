Retinal vessels segmentation
by logistic regression and gradient descent
Isabella Poles, Carlo Amodeo
University of Illinois at Chicago,
{ipoles2,camode2}@uic.edu
Abstract— Assessment of the blood vessel morphology in
retinal fundus images is an indispensable method for diag-
nosing diseases such as diabetic retinopathy and glaucoma.
Ophthalmologists commonly evaluate fundus images with manual
planimetry vessel extraction, which represents a clear bottleneck
and is prone to human errors. Therefore, an automatic vessel
segmentation tool can help clinicians perform this task, thus,
improving the accuracy of the diagnosis. This work proposes a
fully automatic segmentation method based on logistic regression
pixelwise classiﬁcation for deﬁning blood vessels in retinal images
using gradient descent as loss function minimization strategy.
Moreover, custom pre-processing workﬂows facilitate the subse-
quent segmentation depending on the analyzed images’ intensity-
based characteristics. The effectiveness of the proposed method
was evaluated on the benchmark dataset STARE. Compared
with the built-in logistic regression segmentation strategy, our
segmentation outputs resemble in a close manner the ground
truth images. Our logistic regression implementation method
achieves an accuracy of 61.36% as compared to the built-in
strategy which reaches the 60.04%.
I. INTRODUCTION
In the last few years, medical practice is seeing a shift from
a pure human-based assessment of pathologies to computer-
based smart solutions that alleviates some repetitive tasks typ-
ically borne by physicians. To further push towards computer-
assisted medicine, several attentions have been posed, in
ophthalmology, to the study of the blood vessel appearance [1].
Indeed, the retina’s vasculature is a collection of arteries and
veins, which represents crucial morphological structures for
assessing the presence and severity of numerous diseases such
as diabetic retinopathy, hypertension, glaucoma, hemorrhages,
vein occlusion, and neovascularization in fundus images. These
images are commonly acquired with particular cameras -
fundus camera - and are used to analyze the retinal vessels’ di-
ameter and tortuosity [2]. Normally, ophthalmologists rely on
manual planimetry vessel extraction to analyze fundus images.
Unfortunately, this process represents a clear bottleneck and is
prone to human errors considering the vast amount of acquired
images and the possible different pathologies. Based on these
considerations, a reliable automated segmentation method able
to preserve relevant characteristics of retinal blood vessels
could be attractive as Computer-Aided Diagnostic (CAD) tool.
Because of this, the industry is pushing towards automatic
analysis frameworks which signiﬁcantly reduce ophthalmolo-
gists’ workload, facilitating a more effective screening process,
thus, reducing healthcare system costs. Early diagnosis would
prevent patients from reaching severity in vision impairments,
reduce age-related diseases and decrease the medical care
cost [3]. For these reasons, the literature shows multiple
approaches that try to achieve accurate retinal blood vessel
segmentation by image enhancement, energy minimization
problems and machine learning techniques [4]. However, due
to the nature of the image acquisition system, fundus images
show different conditions of illumination, resolution, ﬁeld of
view (FOV), and overlapping tissues in the retina, leading to
signiﬁcant degradation of the performance of automated blood
vessels segmentation tools.
Within this context, we propose a framework to identify and
segment retinal vessels, based on pixel-wise binary classiﬁca-
tion machine learning technique. Our framework provides a
supervised machine learning technique is based on logistic
regression and loss minimization with gradient descent. The
main contribution of this work are:
• design and implementation of an automatic pipeline for
retinal vessel segmentation (Section III-D);
• design and implementation from scratch of custom fea-
tures extraction method for preprocessed fundus images
to deal with the most common artifacts present in them
(Section III-E);
• performance comparison with commonly implemented
logistic regression built-in function (Section IV-A) on the
popular STARE dataset [5].
The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section
II reviews the previous studies on blood vessel segmentation.
Section III provides the detailed description of the imple-
mented retinal vessels segmentation workﬂow, describing the
feature extraction strategy and the logistic regression with
gradient descent iterative algorithm. Section IV presents the
experimental results, while conclusions are drawn in Section
V.
II. RELATED WORK
The most recent and innovative vessel segmentation tech-
niques have shown that the preferred workﬂow for automated
blood vessels segmentation in fundus images relies on prepro-
cessing steps and contrast enhancement. These strategies are
usually implemented considering the luminosity and contrast
variations that occur in the images. These phenomena are
caused by non-ideal acquisition processes that reduce the
fundus image quality due to image acquisition artifacts or
to patient-dependent peculiarities. For instance, non-uniform
illumination, caused by mechanical issues can be corrected
using a combination of techniques that allow achieving higher
segmentation accuracy. Examples are low pass and Gaussian
ﬁltering [6], removal of the haze from the image in the
inverted intensity domain [7], linear contrast stretching process
with a tunable parameter [8], and vessel enhancement with
morphological operations [9]. On the other hand, the core of
the procedure consists of the segmentation process that, based
on several reviews [4], can be performed by four main meth-
ods, namely vessel enhancement, deformable models, tracking
methods, and machine learning methods. Vessel enhancement
approaches improve the quality of vessel perception increasing
their contrast compared to the background and other non-
informative structures. A thresholding step follows this pro-
cedure to obtain the vessel binary mask directly. However,
this approach does not result in a continuous map of the vas-
culature. Examples to overcome this problem include matched
ﬁltering [10], piecewise threshold probing [5], and multi-
scale vesselness ﬁltering [11]. Deformable segmentation
models consider the inﬂuence of internal and external forces
designed to move and deform curves or surfaces. Internal
forces preserve smooth surfaces during the deformation, while
external ones attract the surfaces toward the vessel boundaries.
A parametric description of a surface has been proposed
by [12], while in [13], to overcome the problem of noisy
and non-uniform intensity images, the deformable curve was
constrained to move in statistically homogeneous regions of
fore and background. Deformable models generally consider
the edge parameters, but neglect other image features. Another
approach to segment connected vascular trees is the blood
vessel tracking method, which consists of the deﬁnition of
seed points followed by a growth process guided by image-
derived constraints. Seed points can be either manually deﬁned
or obtained through vessel enhancement approaches [14].
Drawbacks of these approaches are their proclivity for ter-
mination at branch points, which cannot be easily detected
by 1-D ﬁlters, and their reliance upon oversimpliﬁed methods
for locating starting points. Finally, an established literature
describes new advancements in supervised and unsupervised
machine learning methods to segment the retinal vessels.
Recent works show how supervised methods based on deep
learning architectures have surpassed all other automatic tech-
niques, obtaining performance very close to trained human ob-
servers [2]. However they need non-trivial pre-labeled datasets
and ground truth segmentation for training. While between
the unsupervised ones, the matched ﬁlters-based, morphology-
based, and model-based methods are able to identify vessel
and non-vessel pixels by describing hidden arrangements of
image derived features, without any prior knowledge, but being
however more computational intensive [15].
In this context, this work segments retinal vessels exploiting
the binary classiﬁcation strategy implemented using the logis-
tic regression method. In detail, features extraction methods
allows to create the data matrix, while the implementation
from scratch of the gradient descent iterative algorithm allows
the cross entropy loss function minimization. Finally, the per-
formance of the proposed pipeline is evaluated using metrics

III. PROPOSED FRAMEWORK
This section presents an in-depth description of the proposed
workﬂow. The chosen approach has been a machine-learning
based one, as the aim of this work was to deﬁne a complete
pipeline which could, in an automatic way, proceed in the
segmentation of the vessel’s morphology. Consequently, this
section is going to be focused onto these main arguments:
dataset description, input images division, preprocessing of
the images, choice of the model to perform the pixel-wise
classiﬁcation and choice of the features with which proceeding
in feeding the model.
A. Experimental setup
To validate the proposed framework, we employed
STARE [5], a publicly available dataset, which also provides
the ground truth of the vessels for analyzing blood vessel
segmentation algorithms’ performance. STARE stands for
STructured Analysis of the Retina, and 400 retinal images
compose it, captured using TOPCON TRV-50 fundus camera
at 35° FOV. Each slide was digitized to produce a 605x700
pixel image, with 24-bits pixels (standard RGB). Out of the
400 images STARE makes available only 20 retinal fundus
slides with the respective ground truth hand-labeled images,
where ten of them contain pathology that obscures or confuses
the blood vessel appearance in varying portions of the image.
The 20 images, presenting the ground truth, have been divided
into the three groups based on their characteristics, obtaining
a split of 12, 5, and 3 for the informative, low contrast, and
artifacts group, respectively. All the experiments are performed
using Python 3.0 on a laptop (2.5GHz Intel Core i5-7200U
CPU, 8GB RAM).
B. Data splitting
To deal with the difference in the image quality the input
images are manually classiﬁed into three groups based on
intensity features such as contrast, non-uniform illumination
effects, and presence of artifacts due to the non-idealities in
the acquisition process. The informative group consists of
images with high contrasted blood vessels compared to the
background and absence of illumination artifacts. The low
contrast group images are characterized by the presence of

C. Pre-processing
The ﬁrst step of the pre-processing has been the splitting
the colored RGB input retinal image into Red, Green, and
Blue color channels. Only the Green band has then been
processed, due to the fact that the Blue band in a retinal
image is often empty, while the Red band is often saturated;
consequently, the vessels and the background contrast is much
clearer in the Green channel. Moreover, considering just one
of the three channels has led to a faster training procedure
for the created model. Going on, a simple normalization
of the images has been performed: the minimum and the
maximum brightness for each image have been computed;
the minimum has been subtracted by each image, and then
it has been divided by its maximum. For what regards the
label images, the normalization has been computed as follows:
each pixel with a value different from 0 has been set to 1.
Once this second step has been performed, the next one is the
application of a morphological transformation to the images.
The chosen one is the white top-hat transformation, which is
performed through the difference between the original image
and its morphological opening with respect to a structural
disk element, γ, with a radius of 30 pixels. The results of
the preprocessing can be seen in Figure 2.

the representation of an image into something that is more
meaningful and easier to analyse. In this work, the aim was
to highlight the retinal vessels given in the STARE dataset.

learning one; more speciﬁcally, it has been decided to work
with a supervised learning algorithm, which could lead to

IV. RESULTS
This Section presents an evaluation of the reached accuracy
performance and a comparison with the built-in Python logistic
regression function.
A. Accuracy evaluation
The resulting segmentation is compared to the ground
truth hand-labeled images provided by the STARE dataset, to
assess the proposed method’s performance on an image taken
from the informative image group. A further comparison is
performed with the results achieved with the built-in logistic
regression function. The visual results are shown for the
informative test image that has shown the best segmentation
performance Figure 6. From the comparison between the
ground truth images and the obtained results with both the
from scratch logistic regression implementation and the built-
in one, shown in Figure 6, it is possible to appreciate how
our segmentation algorithm is able to reach an accuracy
very close to the one achieved with the logistic regression
built-in strategy. Both the implemented methods maintain the
original width of the majority of the vessels, reaching a
good accuracy. The Intersection Over Union (IoU), the Dice
Similarity Coefﬁcient (DSC) and the Accuracy score (Acc)
values are analyzed to provide a quantitative assessment of our
method’s performance compared to manual segmentation and
the other automatic segmentation strategy [16]. Such metrics

that external FOV pixels contribute equally in both cases. The

V. CONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
This work has introduced an automated and low-cost
framework for segmenting retinal blood vessels in fundus
images, based on implementing the logistic regression binary
classiﬁcation method using gradient descent as loss function
minimization strategy. The pre-processing steps have been
specialized based on the video intensity characteristics of
the STARE dataset images to deal with the illumination
non-homogeneities and the artifacts’ presence. Experimental
results, focused on the DSC, Acc and IoU values, revealed
that the proposed method is accurate in extracting retinal
vessels also with the presence of illumination variations. We
consider this work to be a step towards improvements in the
implementation of features extraction methods for automatic
image classiﬁcation, reﬁnements in the pre-processing steps,
especially in correspondence of artifacts, and computational
cost reduction. We believe that all these improvements will
lead to the implementation and the adoption of efﬁcient
solutions in pathologies’ prevention and diagnosis.
